# Voice — Credex Copy

> Tonal frame for every section of every page. Cross-references `plan/01.Brand-Analysis.md` §Tone of Voice.

## Three words

**Bold · Trustworthy · Empowering**

Optional 4th: *Movement / Disruptive*.

## Voice do's

- **Direct, founder-driven, no-nonsense.** Kendall's stated preference — no consultant-speak.
- **Lead with quantified financial proof** in the visitor's own volume language. (See per-audience volume notes in `audiences/<vertical>.md`.)
- **Frame CredX as a structural shift** — a *movement*, not a feature, not a SaaS add-on.
- **Canadian-first business tone.** Alberta / BC / Ontario soft launch. Pan-Canadian, business-conservative, professional.
- **Founder voice is allowed.** Direct quotes from Kendall or named team members carry the page. Use sparingly and verbatim.

## Voice don'ts

- **Don't hedge or apologise** for the ambition. CredX is "picking a fight with Visa and MasterCard" — own it.
- **Don't use government / political language.** Avoid regional political signals.
- **Don't bury the movement narrative** in the footer or in a tagline. It must lead.
- **Don't pile information without hierarchy.** Kendall: *"If I have to read pages and pages, it's not so good."*
- **Don't use US-centric idioms** in the Canadian-first version. Save those for the US-pipeline pages (later phase).
- **Don't use marketing clichés** ("revolutionary," "game-changing," "best-in-class," "next-generation"). The numbers do the work.
- **Don't paraphrase legal disclaimers** (Tier 1/2/3 from Brand Guide §4) — locked text.
- **Don't write at the level of consumer marketing.** The audience is senior B2B decision-makers (VP+, C-suite).

## Register

- **Reading level:** senior B2B decision-maker — VP Operations, CFO, CMO, VP Partnerships.
- **Attention budget:** 30 seconds for the first screen. Anything that doesn't pull weight there goes below the fold.
- **Sentence length:** mix. Short declarations carry the page. Use longer sentences only when the point requires it.
- **Voice person:** second-person address to the merchant/operator (`Your dealership` / `Your venue` / `Your team` / `Your platform`). Avoid third-person abstractions (`merchants benefit`).

## AI patterns to obliterate (D&Z internal style guide)

> Source: `documents/04_Style-Guide/AI Stuff We Need to Obliterate V.2.docx`. These are the AI-copy tells D&Z has explicitly flagged across all clients. **Apply globally to every Credex section.**

| # | Pattern | Why it's banned | Do this instead |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Overused / misused em dashes** — especially in H1/H2/titles. *e.g., "Real-time insights—made easy."* | Signals unclear sentence structure; the writer couldn't decide how to connect ideas. | Cleaner connectors. Commas. Or just two sentences. |
| 2 | **"Not just X, but Y"** structure. *e.g., "We don't just provide insights, we increase your ROI."* | AI's favorite "balanced and dramatic" cliché. Repeats ideas without adding clarity. | Just say what you do: "We give you insights that improve your ROI." |
| 3 | **"Here's what sets us apart:"** preface. | 10,000 better ways to convey the same thing. | Skip the preface. Lead with the difference itself. |
| 4 | **"From X to Y, we…"** filler. *e.g., "From children to their parents, we're committed to…"* | Tries to sound inclusive; ends up vague and awkward. | Get specific about who and how. |
| 5 | **"X meets Y"** metaphor. *e.g., "Where creativity meets convenience."* | Empty filler. Could belong to any brand in any industry. | Describe the actual combination of qualities directly. |
| 6 | **"Dive / delve in" or "Dive into"** — anywhere. | Overused and lazy. | Rewrite ~100% of the time. (Allowed only as a pun in literally water-related content.) |
| 7 | **H1 / title / CTA built from 2–3 word fragmented sentences.** *e.g., "DataNet Security: Real-Time Updates. Uncompromising Innovation."* | Feels "intense" but reads as vague. Terrible for SEO. Readers skim past. | One clear descriptive line that says what you do. *e.g., "Security software that updates in real time, so you're always protected."* |
| 8 | **Overuse of transitional words.** "Accordingly," "Indeed," "Additionally," "However." | Almost always unnecessary. | Minimize. Usually can be cut without loss. |
| 9 | **Vague nouns.** *innovation, efficiency, landscape, realm, tapestry, transformation, pivotal, tailor.* | Lazy. Says nothing. | Use the specific thing. |
| 10 | **Vague phrases.** *"A testament to…", "In conclusion…", "It's important to note that…", "Unlock / unleash the power…", "In today's rapidly evolving market / world", "Provide / get valuable insights", "To play a crucial role", "… shaping the future of …"* | AI's go-to filler. | Use very infrequently — and never as a section opener. |
| 11 | **Data-client clichés** (relevant for our financial-proof pages). *"Deliver actionable insights", "In-depth data analysis", "Drive insightful data-driven decisions", "Leveraging data-driven insights", "Extract meaningful insights".* | Repetitive, uninteresting fluff. | Say what the number is. Say what it means for the reader. Skip the abstraction. |
| 12 | **Adverb saturation.** *enthusiastically, flawlessly, consistently, efficiently.* | Not 100% banned, but a saturated piece becomes a chore. | Trim ruthlessly. One adverb per paragraph max. |
| 13 | **Triple-quality lists with no content.** *e.g., "Smarter Tools. Faster Growth. Better Results."* | AI signature. Sounds branded; says nothing. | If a triple is right, each item needs concrete content (number, mechanism, named outcome) — not abstractions. |

### Tension with the locked Credex hooks

⚠ Some LOCKED hero sub-copy (`plan/03.Full-Sitemap.md`) uses fragmented patterns that brush against rule #7. Examples:

- *"Instant embedded credit at your dealership F&I desk — no terminals, no card networks, no waiting."* (Automotive S1 sub)
- *"A partnership, not a pitch. One integration, thousands of merchants saving $30M per $100M in volume."* (POS S1 sub)

These are intentionally bold / declarative — Kendall's mandate is "ruthlessly simple." They are **allowed** because each fragment carries concrete content (numbers, mechanisms), not abstract "brand-y" qualities. The rule is: fragmented punchy lines are fine when they're *quantified and concrete*; they're banned when they're vague triples.

**When in doubt:** if a fragment can be removed and the section still says something specific, the fragment was filler — cut it.
